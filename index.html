How the peg pyramid works

The board uses a triangular layout of staggered pegs. Each row is one peg wider than the one above it, so the ball faces a series of left-or-right decisions as it travels downward. By the time it reaches the bottom, it lands in one of the pockets lined up across the base.

Because every bounce is a fresh split in direction, the middle pockets tend to collect more balls than the outer edges. Mathematicians describe the pattern as a Galton board, and the shape of the results follows a binomial curve. In plain terms, the common pockets pay less, while rare edge pockets carry much larger multipliers.
